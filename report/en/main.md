# The Report on AI and Agile in 2030

### 1. Introduction

In 2025, adopting AI in software development is no longer optional—it has become almost standard. According to the latest report from DORA (DevOps Research and Assessment), as many as 90% of organizations use AI in their day-to-day operations. As a result, technology leaders’ attention has shifted from the question of whether to adopt AI to a more advanced strategic challenge: how to effectively integrate AI and maximize its value.

Against this new mindset, this report aims to predict and clarify what agile development will look like in the AI era around 2030. To do so, it brings together the latest survey data, essays from experts actively working on the front lines, and a synthesis of academic roadmaps into a coherent analysis.

This report is intended for technology leaders, people practicing agile every day, and everyone involved in development teams. By exploring how AI will transform not only team structures and each member’s roles, but also daily processes and even agile philosophy itself, this report provides strategic insights to help organizations prepare for the future.

The changes brought by AI are not mere advancements in tools. They represent a paradigm shift across development culture itself—challenging how we work, how we collaborate, and how we create value.

**Key takeaways**

- The primary role of AI is an “**Amplifier**.” AI amplifies both an organization’s existing strengths and weaknesses.
- This shift transforms agile teams into smaller, faster units, making **human-centered skills**—such as empathy and strategic thinking—the source of value creation.
- Success in the AI era does not come from adopting the “best” AI tools, but from investing in the **organizational capabilities** that AI amplifies (such as high-quality internal platforms and a healthy data ecosystem).

### 2. A New Paradigm: AI as an Amplifier and a Teammate

To understand the relationship between AI and agile development in 2030, we need a new perspective on what AI is “for.” The key lies in two powerful metaphors proposed by DORA and many experts: the “**Amplifier**” introduced in the DORA report, and “**Teammate**” described by professionals. These are not competing ideas; rather, they offer complementary lenses for viewing AI-driven transformation.

“Amplifier” is a **system-level** metaphor explaining AI’s impact on an organization-wide output. “Teammate” is a **team-level** metaphor depicting AI’s influence on daily work and roles. Together, these two perspectives form the foundation of any future AI adoption strategy.

### 2.1. AI is an Amplifier of Organizational Capabilities

DORA’s central conclusion is that AI functions as an “amplifier.” This means that AI can strengthen the advantages of high-performing organizations even further, while simultaneously magnifying the dysfunction and confusion of organizations that are already struggling. The strategic implications derived from this insight are extremely important: **the key to getting maximum value from AI investment lies not in AI tools themselves, but in investing in the organizational systems that underpin them**.

Without a solid foundation—such as high-quality internal platforms, clear and efficient workflows, and alignment across teams—localized productivity gains from AI will be absorbed in the chaos of downstream activities and will not translate into organization-wide outcomes. AI is a mirror that reflects and amplifies what an organization is already capable of.

### 2.2. AI is a New Teammate

At the same time, experts such as Henrik Kniberg and Dave West of Scrum.org have presented AI as an “**AI colleague**” or “co-pilot” that takes on tasks like coding, summarizing, and analysis. This partnership between humans and AI enables human team members to focus on more complex, higher-value strategic work—such as creative problem-solving and deep empathy-building with stakeholders.

As Dave West points out, AI lacks human-specific **empathy**, so it cannot fully replace a product owner. However, by assigning tasks such as data analysis and summarizing user feedback, AI can become an excellent partner for the product owner. This new “teammate” complements human capabilities and dramatically raises the team’s overall potential.

Seeing AI not merely as a tool, but as part of a system that amplifies organizational capabilities and works alongside us in daily work—this paradigm shift raises a key question: **what specific changes will it force in team structures and roles?**

### 3. Transforming Agile Teams: Structure, Roles, and Essential Skills

In a future where AI is deeply integrated into development processes as a “teammate,” long-standing assumptions about team size and composition are overturned at the root. Along with this, the responsibilities required for each role will be redefined, and the skill sets essential to team success will change significantly as well.

### 3.1. The Future of Team Structure: Smaller Teams and More Teams

Henrik Kniberg predicts that agile teams in the AI era will become dramatically smaller. Conventional cross-functional teams with diverse skill sets may shift toward small and nimble units like “**1–2 humans + AI**.” Because AI can cover much of the specialist knowledge, knowledge bottlenecks can be reduced, enabling autonomous value creation even with fewer people.

Interestingly, this shift leads to a seemingly paradoxical outcome. While individual teams become smaller, the organization’s overall development capacity increases—allowing companies to form more teams. As a result, **the number of teams grows, and the importance of coordination and alignment between teams is amplified more than ever.** Agile practice will move its focus from within teams to across teams.

### 3.2. Redefining Roles

As collaboration with AI becomes the norm, the core responsibilities of agile team roles shift substantially.

| Role | Conventional role | Role in the AI era of 2030 |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Developer** | Implementer of code, spending much time on manual coding. | Transforms into the **architect, prompt engineer, and the final reviewer responsible for AI-generated code**. As a practitioner of “Programming with Trust,” the role critically verifies AI outputs and ensures overall system quality and security. |
| **Product Owner** | Spends a lot of time on tactical backlog management and requirements organization. | Delegates data analysis and summarization of vast amounts of user feedback to AI, and focuses instead on more human activities: **strategy, empathy formation with stakeholders, and coordinating complex human relationships.** |
| **Scrum Master / Agile Coach** | Strongly acts as a “teacher,” instructing teams in basic agile practices and how to run events. | Central focus becomes the “coach” role: maximizing productivity and collaboration in a hybrid team consisting of humans and AI, and developing human-only skills. Rather than teaching tool usage, it contributes to **improving the team’s learning capability and adaptability.** |

### 3.3. Essential Human-Centered Skills in 2030: Your Moat

Stefan Wolpers describes AI as an “**Expertise Detector**.” AI automates mechanical tasks and information processing, but it cannot replace human beings who possess true expertise—judgment rooted in experience and deep insight. In the AI era, what becomes the source of each individual’s competitive advantage is human-centered skills that AI cannot imitate: the “**Moat**” that protects one’s career.

As we move toward 2030, the following skills become indispensable:

- **Empathy**: the ability to deeply understand users’ and stakeholders’ emotions and positions, and to grasp true needs.
- **The ability to read the room**: in meetings and negotiations, sensing unspoken nuances and dynamics, and guiding dialogue.
- **Leadership for psychological safety**: creating an environment where team members can speak honestly, propose ideas, and take challenges without fearing failure.
- **Strategic negotiation**: aligning complex interests and forming agreements that benefit the organization as a whole.
- **Creativity**: the ability to critically evaluate AI-generated options and combine them to create entirely new value.

With roles and skill sets changing in this way, how should we revisit day-to-day agile practices?

### 4. Re-evaluating Agile Practices and Events

The transformation of team structures and roles also calls for reconsideration of the practices and events at the core of Scrum. Dramatic improvements in development speed introduced by AI challenge the traditional sense of time (cadence) and the purposes of each event. While agile principles remain unchanged, the way we implement them must evolve flexibly.

### 4.1. The Transformation of Sprints: Shorter, or a Shift Toward Flow

As implementation work is no longer the bottleneck, the need for sprints spanning weeks fades. As Henrik Kniberg and Ryutaro Yoshiba predict, sprint duration may shrink from one week to several days—and in extreme cases, to **daily cycles**. This change offers a tremendous benefit: **accelerating feedback loops**, enabling teams to iterate on hypothesis testing more quickly.

However, this acceleration creates new challenges. It may become difficult for busy stakeholders to participate in every sprint review. As a result, **the frequency of stakeholder review participation may become a new bottleneck**—a somewhat ironic situation where AI **amplifies** existing constraints rather than eliminating them.

### 4.2. How Work Gets Done: Shifting from Parallel Work to Mob Work

As developers shift from being code implementers to taking responsibility for reviews (Section 3.2), the rationality of parallel work by individuals declines, and the team-wide consensus-building process becomes the new bottleneck. As Yoshiba points out, the dramatic improvement in implementation speed provided by AI shifts the bottleneck from “writing code” to **“consensus building and review.”**

One effective approach to address this new bottleneck is to **re-evaluate the value of mob programming**. With everyone looking at the same screen, the team reviews AI-generated code in real time and performs synchronized clarification of specifications and consensus building. This approach dramatically reduces rework later and optimizes the team’s overall flow.

### 4.3. Re-evaluating Documentation: From “Working Software” to “High-Quality Input for AI”

The famous agile manifesto value “Working software over comprehensive documentation” forces a new interpretation in the AI era. Documentation—the instructions that serve as input to AI—is crucial to drive AI effectively.

Text-based documentation that is free from ambiguity and clearly describes assumptions functions as **high-quality input for AI**, significantly affecting the quality of generated code. Ironically, a reversal is taking place: the value of documentation has never been higher. The era in which the ability to write good documentation directly translates into team productivity is now arriving.

### 4.4. The Reduced Importance of Estimation

As the time required for implementation shortens significantly, and interactive processes with AI become central—processes that are difficult to predict—traditional estimation methods such as story points decline in importance. Instead of spending time estimating the effort of implementation, teams and stakeholders should spend more time **building deep consensus about the purpose (Why) and content (What) of product backlog items.** Value exploration becomes the team’s primary activity.

These evolutions in practice cannot be achieved by teams alone. So what organizational systems are essential to maximize AI’s potential?

### 5. Systems Are the Key: Organizational Factors That Maximize AI Potential

DORA’s core message, emphasized repeatedly, is: **the success of AI is a problem of systems, not of tools.** AI acts as a catalyst that **amplifies** an organization’s existing systems and culture. To convert the potential productivity gains from AI into sustainable organizational competitive advantage, technical and cultural foundations that support AI tools are essential. This section details the key organizational capabilities required to maximize the impact of AI investment.

### 5.1. The DORA AI Capability Model: Seven Practices That Amplify Success

DORA identifies seven organizational capabilities that have been demonstrated to amplify the benefits of AI and presents them as the “DORA AI Capability Model.” These capabilities are the targets on which AI-as-an-amplifier acts, and building them is the key to successful AI adoption.

1. **A clear and widely communicated AI stance**: define organizational policies on AI usage (acceptable tools, how data privacy is handled, etc.) and communicate them to all employees. This allows developers to experiment with confidence and manage risks more easily.
2. **A healthy data ecosystem**: possess high-quality, accessible, integrated internal data foundations. Because AI models learn from data, the health of the data ecosystem directly impacts output quality.
3. **Internal data that AI can access**: build mechanisms for AI tools to safely access organizational internal data (code repositories, documents, and more). This enables AI to understand organization-specific context and provide more accurate support.
4. **Strong version control practices**: mature version control practices such as frequent commits and easy rollback. These are essential to ensure the psychological safety of experimentation in the face of accelerated and increased volume of changes driven by AI.
5. **Working in small batches**: a practice of splitting work into small units and integrating/reviewing them quickly. This makes it easier to manage review load for AI-generated code and accelerates feedback loops.
6. **User-centered focus**: a culture in which team activities are always directed toward creating value for end users. This ensures that AI-driven productivity gains lead to improvements that truly benefit users—not self-satisfying feature development.
7. **High-quality internal platforms**: platforms that provide developers with excellent developer experience, enabling self-service access to the tools and infrastructure they need.

### 5.2. The Strategic Importance of High-Quality Internal Platforms

DORA’s findings highlight the importance of platform engineering especially. High-quality internal platforms function as the “**delivery and governance layer**” essential for scaling individual productivity improvements enabled by AI into organization-wide impact. An excellent platform becomes the engine that supports safe and efficient organization-wide rollout of AI tools and spreads the benefits of AI adoption across the enterprise.

### 5.3. The Role of Value Stream Management (VSM)

Value stream management (VSM) is an approach to visualize and optimize the flow of work from ideas to customer value. DORA’s report shows that VSM works as a “**force multiplier**” for AI. Even if coding speed in development teams doubles after adopting AI, the overall value delivery speed does not change if bottlenecks remain in review or deployment processes. By taking a system-level perspective provided by VSM, we can prevent localized productivity gains from being absorbed by downstream bottlenecks and connect them to organization-wide value creation.

If these organizational capabilities are not in place, adopting AI may not only fail to deliver expected benefits—it may also create new risks and unprecedented challenges. So what challenges should we prepare for?

### 6. New Frontiers and Major Challenges Toward 2030

AI offers immense potential, but integrating AI into development processes reveals major challenges that we must actively address to ensure a sustainable and safe future. Behind technological progress, human aspects such as reliability, talent development, and ethics are emerging as new frontiers.

### 6.1. Trust, Verification, and Security: A New Common Sense for “Programming with Trust”

As “The Roadmap for Software Engineering in 2030” and reports from NIST (the U.S. National Institute of Standards and Technology) indicate, code generated by AI contains different types of error modes than traditional bugs. Issues such as generating code based on false facts due to hallucinations, and introducing bias caused by training data, cannot be adequately addressed by classical verification and validation (V&V) approaches alone. DORA’s report also supports this: it reports that a notable **30% of respondents do not trust AI-generated code at all, or barely trust it.**

To address this challenge, a new concept has been proposed: **“Programming with Trust.”** Rather than blindly trusting AI outputs, this development style assumes continuous human critical oversight and verification. To manage vulnerabilities introduced unintentionally by AI, the importance of thorough code review and DevSecOps practices that embed security across the entire development process increases even further.

### 6.2. The Talent Development Crisis: Acquiring Skills for Next-Generation Developers

As Ryutaro Yoshiba and Matt Beane point out, one of the most serious long-term risks in the AI era is talent development. The question **“In a world where AI automates most coding work, how will early-career developers acquire practical skills and grow into competent senior engineers?”** is an urgent challenge for all technical organizations.

The traditional training model of OJT (On-the-Job Training) through daily coding work may cease to function. To confront this issue seriously, organizations must strategically invest in new training frameworks and skill development programs—such as pair programming, mob programming, and intentionally creating learning opportunities.

### 6.3. Accountability and Ethics: AI Cannot Be an Excuse

No matter how advanced an AI system becomes, ultimate **accountability for its output always remains with humans.** When product malfunctions or discriminatory judgments by AI become problems, explanations such as “AI decided so” will never be accepted.

For this reason, ensuring transparency—recording and documenting AI-assisted decision-making processes so they can be traced later—is crucial. In addition, designing ethical considerations such as fairness and privacy from the design stage and building mechanisms to ensure them is no longer optional; it is a mandatory requirement for developing trustworthy products.

These challenges are not insurmountable walls. Then what kind of future awaits us beyond overcoming them?

### 7. Conclusion: The Future Is Human-Centered—and Reinforced by AI

What this report reveals is that agile development in 2030 will not be a dystopian future where agile and humans are replaced by AI. Instead, both will deeply transform and coexist and cooperate in new forms. AI will not end agile; it will act as a catalyst that elevates its essence to the next stage.

We predicted that the following major changes will occur:

- Teams will become smaller, faster, and more numerous, making coordination across teams a new focal point.
- The roles of developers, product owners, and Scrum Masters will be redefined to assume collaboration with AI, giving decisive value to human-centered skills such as empathy and strategic thinking.
- Agile practices themselves—such as shorter sprints, the recommendation of mob work, and re-evaluating high-quality documentation—will be reconsidered with human-AI collaboration in mind.

As DORA’s report and Stefan Wolpers strongly demonstrate, AI is a “**mirror**” that reflects an organization’s existing capabilities and amplifies them. AI can lift teams that are already practicing well to even greater heights, while also making the problems of struggling teams more severe.

Therefore, what truly enables a leap in the AI era is not using AI as a substitute for humans, but leveraging it as a partner for achieving higher levels of performance and value delivery—along with making wise investments in people and systems. The future is an exciting frontier in which AI **amplifies** human abilities and becomes more **human-centered**. What is asked of leaders is whether they are willing to take responsibility for designing that future through investment in their people and systems, rather than simply observing the change.
